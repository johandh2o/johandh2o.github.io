Let us suppose you have a large dataset where you collected data from indiduals and their contexts: age, sex, weight, BMI, symptoms, results from lab tests, past diagnoses, whether they received some kind of treatment, and the outcome of that treatment. The treatment could be, for example, certain anti-diabetic medication, and the outcome could be the level of blood sugar.

This is an example of an  **observational** or **nonexperimental study**, where interventions (treatments) have been recorded along contexts and outcomes, but they were not randomly assigned in the sample of individuals. Observatuional studies are widespread in health sciences and economics, but they present challenges to **causal inference**, since the treatment assignment might be endogenous. This means that the treatment is not independent from the context, thus many correlation-based estimators of the treatment impact might carry a sensible bias.

Inferring causal impacts is a fundamental problem in science and economics, because it allows us to answer why-questions in terms of cause and effect, avoiding answers based on spurious relations. It also allows us to apply **counterfactual reasoning** to determine the best course of action. If the outcome of patient $i$ taking treatment $A$ is better than *the outcome that would ocurr had the same patient received treatment $B$*, then we would recommend her treatment $A$ with some confidence.

So, how can we learn from observational data which treatment would lead to a better outcome for a given context? If the outcome is a simple, for example linear, function of the context and treatment, then there is a plethora of methodologies to infer causal effects. Most of them based on regression or tree ensembles, and they work only on the factual regime; that is to say, with the observed data.

Observed variables: the data
------

$$
\begin{aligned}
    & X = (x_i)_{i=1}^n,\ x_i\in\mathcal{X} & x_i \text{ context/feature of obs. } i\\
    & T = (t_i)_{i=1}^n,\ t_i\in\{0,1\} & t_i \text{ treatment for obs. } i\\
    & Y^F = (y_i^F)_{i=1}^n,\ y^F_i\in\mathcal{Y} & y_i^F \text{ factual outcome of obs. } i
\end{aligned}
$$

Unobserved/latent variables in Rubin's framework
------

$$
\begin{aligned}
    & Y_t(x_i)\in\mathcal{Y} &\text{ Potential outcome for } i \text{ under } t_i=t\in\{0,1\}  \\
    &\text{ITE}(x_i) = Y_1(x_i)-Y_0(x_i) & \text{ Individualized Treatment Effect for } i \\
    & \text{ATE}=\mathbb{E}_{x\sim P}\text{ITE}(x) & \text{ Average Treatment Effect} 
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
  y_i^F = t_iY_1(x_i)+(1-t_i)Y_0(x_i)
\end{aligned}
$$

Learned functions
------

$$
\begin{aligned}
    & \Phi:\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^d \qquad\qquad\quad \Phi(x_i) \text{ is the representation of context } x_i \text{ into } \mathbb{R}^d\\
    & h:\mathbb{R}^d\times\{0,1\}\rightarrow\mathcal{Y} \qquad h(\Phi(x_i),t_i)\approx y_i,\ h \text{ is the learner}\\
    &\hat{\text{ITE}}(x_i) = (2t_i-1)\left[y_i^F-h(\Phi(x_i),1-t_i)\right],\quad \hat{\text{ATE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \hat{\text{ITE}}(x_i)
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
  j(i) \in \arg\min_{j\in [n], t_j=1-t_i}d(x_j, x_j)
\end{aligned}
$$


Factual and counterfactual empirical feature distributions given $\Phi$
------

$$
\begin{aligned}
    & \hat{P}^F_\Phi = \{(\Phi(x_i),t_i\}_{i=1}^n & \text{ Factual empirical feature distribution}\\
    & \hat{P}^C_\Phi = \{(\Phi(x_i),1-t_i\}_{i=1}^n & \text{ Counterfactual empirical feature distribution}\\
\end{aligned}
$$

Discrepancy measure
------

$$
\begin{aligned}
    \text{disc}_{\mathcal{H}}(\hat{P}^F_\Phi, \hat{P}^C_\Phi) = \max_{\beta,\beta'\in \mathcal{H}}\abs{\mathbb{E}_{z\sim \hat{P}^F_\Phi}L(\beta(z),\beta'(z))-\mathbb{E}_{z\sim \hat{P}^C_\Phi}L(\beta(z),\beta'(z))}
\end{aligned}
$$

Objective functions
------

$$
\begin{aligned}
    B_1(\Phi, h) &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\abs{h(\Phi(x_i),t_i)-y^F_i} \quad  \alert{\text{ Robust min. of factual errors}} \\
    & +\frac{\gamma}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\abs{h(\Phi(x_i),1-t_i)-y^F_{j(i)}} \quad \alert{\text{ Robust min. of counterfactual e.}}  \\
    & +\alpha \text{disc}_{\mathcal{H}}(\hat{P}^F_\Phi, \hat{P}^C_\Phi) \quad \alert{\text{ Balancing factual/counter distributions of rep.}} \\
    B_2(\Phi, h) &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\left[h(\Phi(x_i),t_i)-y^F_i\right]^2 \quad \alert{\text{ Minimization of factual errors}} \\
    & + \lambda \norm{h}_{\mathcal{H}} \alert{\text{ Regularization}} 
\end{aligned}
$$

The algorithm
------
$$
\begin{aligned}
    (\Phi^*,g^*) &= \arg\min_{\Phi\in\mathcal{N}, g\in\mathcal{H}}B_1(\Phi, g) & \text{ Optimal robust and balanced rep.}\\
    h^* &= \arg\min_{h\in\mathcal{H}}B_2(\Phi^*, h) & \text{ Optimal final learner}
\end{aligned}
$$
