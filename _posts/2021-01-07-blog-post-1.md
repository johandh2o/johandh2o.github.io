Let us suppose you have a large dataset where you collected data from indiduals and their contexts: age, sex, weight, BMI, symptoms, results from lab tests, past diagnoses, whether they received some kind of treatment, and the outcome of that treatment. The treatment could be, for example, certain anti-diabetic medication, and the outcome could be the level of blood sugar.

This is an example of an  **observational** or **nonexperimental study**, where interventions (treatments) have been recorded along contexts and outcomes, but they were not randomly assigned to the sample of individuals. Observational studies are widespread in health sciences and economics, but they present challenges to **causal inference**: the observed treatment assignment might be endogenous, and not independent from the context. Thus, many correlation-based estimators of the treatment impact might carry a sensible bias.

Inferring causal impacts is a fundamental problem in science and economics. It it allows us to answer why-questions in terms of cause and effect, avoiding answers based on spurious relations. It also allows us to apply **counterfactual reasoning** to determine the best course of action. If the estimated outcome for patient $i$ taking treatment $A$ is better than *the outcome that would ocurr had the same patient received treatment $B$*, then we would recommend her treatment $A$ with some confidence.

So, how can we learn from observational data which treatment would lead to a better outcome for a given context? If the outcome is a simple, for example linear, function of the context and the treatment, then there is a plethora of methodologies to infer causal effects. Most of them are based on regression or tree ensembles, carry strong assumptions and work only on the factual regime (with the observed data).

**Johansson, Shalit & Sontag (2016)** paper: *Learning representations for counterfactual Inference* presents the case where the outcome is a highly complex function of the context and treatment, a complex representation of the former is requiered, and there is some need to balance results between the factual and counterfactual regimes: 

> [*Learning representations for counterfactual Inference*, by Johansson, Shalit & Sontag (2016)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1605.03661)
> Proceedings of the 33 rd International Conference on Machine Learning, New York, NY, USA, 2016. JMLR: W&CP volume 48. Copyright 2016 by the author(s).

The main ideas from this paper are:
1. The problem of counterfactual inferece is related to those of domain adpation and representation learning, as they look for some degree of invariance:
  1. In domain knoledge: invariance to the identification of data source. In particular, invariance to the shift of covariate
  2. In fair representations: invariance to the identification of populations that may be in social disadvantage
  3. In counterfactual inference: invariance to the identification of the treatment and the representation of context (the features)
2. In the case of binary treatments, $t\in\{0,1\}$, a representation that delivers some degree of similarity between the factual empirical distribution of the features (treatment-representations of context) and the counterfactual empirical distribution (the hypothetical feature data that we would have observed had every individual received the opposite treatment) can be achieved by optimizing an objective function consisting of:
  1. Minimization of the robust $L_1$ prediction errors in the factual regime
  2. Minimization of the robust $L_1$ prediction errors in the counterfactual regime, where the estimated counterfactual outcome can be estimated using the outcome of the nearest neighbor with the opposite treatment
  3. A form of regularization that directly penalizes imbalances between these distributions, using the discrepancy measure 
3. The proposed method approximately minimizes an upper bound on a regret term in the counterfactual regime, and it achieve state-of-the art performance in simulations

Observed variables: the data
------
Let $[n] =\{1,...,n\}$ be the sample of individuals or observations in our observational study. We can define the observed variables:
$$
\begin{aligned}
    & X = (x_i)_{i=1}^n,\ x_i\in\mathcal{X} & x_i \text{ context/feature of obs. } i\\
    & T = (t_i)_{i=1}^n,\ t_i\in\{0,1\} & t_i \text{ treatment for obs. } i\\
    & Y^F = (y_i^F)_{i=1}^n,\ y^F_i\in\mathcal{Y} & y_i^F \text{ factual outcome of obs. } i
\end{aligned}
$$

Unobserved/latent variables in Rubin's framework
------
We can also define some unobserved variables in Rubin's framework of potential outcomes, which allow us to to define directly the ITE and ATE
$$
\begin{aligned}
    & Y_t(x_i)\in\mathcal{Y} &\text{ Potential outcome for } i \text{ under } t_i=t\in\{0,1\}  \\
    &\text{ITE}(x_i) = Y_1(x_i)-Y_0(x_i) & \text{ Individualized Treatment Effect for } i \\
    & \text{ATE}=\mathbb{E}_{x\sim P}\text{ITE}(x) & \text{ Average Treatment Effect} 
\end{aligned}
$$

The relationship between the potential outcomes and factual outcomes is the following. If individual $i$ with context $x_i$ receives the treatment ($t_i=1$), then their potential outcome under treatment collapses into their factual outome ($y_i^F=Y_1(x_i)$). If they do not receive the treatment ($t_i=0$), then their potential outcome under no-treatment collapses into their factual outome ($y_i^F=Y_0(x_i)$)
$$
\begin{aligned}
  y_i^F = t_iY_1(x_i)+(1-t_i)Y_0(x_i)
\end{aligned}
$$

Learned functions
------
For a fixed sample, the representation fuction of the context, $Phi$, and the learner $h$, are functions which can be learned from the data. They can be used to esimate the ITE and ATE:
$$
\begin{aligned}
    & \Phi:\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^d \qquad\qquad\quad \Phi(x_i) \text{ is the representation of context } x_i \text{ into } \mathbb{R}^d\\
    & h:\mathbb{R}^d\times\{0,1\}\rightarrow\mathcal{Y} \qquad h(\Phi(x_i),t_i)\approx y_i,\ h \text{ is the learner}\\
    &\hat{\text{ITE}}(x_i) = (2t_i-1)\left[y_i^F-h(\Phi(x_i),1-t_i)\right],\quad \hat{\text{ATE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \hat{\text{ITE}}(x_i)
\end{aligned}
$$

Estimation of the counterfactual outcome
------
What would the outcome be for individual $i$ with context $x_i$ had he received the opposite treatment? Let us look at the outcome of the other individual with opposite treatment and nearest context:
$$
\begin{aligned}
  j(i) \in \arg\min_{j\in [n], t_j=1-t_i}d(x_j, x_j)
\end{aligned}
$$

Then $y_{j(i)}$ could serve as the counterfactual estimation of the outcome of $i$

Factual and counterfactual empirical feature distributions 
------
Given the representation function $Phi$, we can define the factual and counterfactual feature distributions as: 
$$
\begin{aligned}
    & \hat{P}^F_\Phi = \{(\Phi(x_i),t_i\}_{i=1}^n & \text{ Factual empirical feature distribution}\\
    & \hat{P}^C_\Phi = \{(\Phi(x_i),1-t_i\}_{i=1}^n & \text{ Counterfactual empirical feature distribution}\\
\end{aligned}
$$

Discrepancy measure
------
The discrepancy distance measure the non-similarity between the factual and counterfactual feature distributions 
$$
\begin{aligned}
    \text{disc}_{\mathcal{H}}(\hat{P}^F_\Phi, \hat{P}^C_\Phi) = \max_{\beta,\beta'\in \mathcal{H}}\abs{\mathbb{E}_{z\sim \hat{P}^F_\Phi}L(\beta(z),\beta'(z))-\mathbb{E}_{z\sim \hat{P}^C_\Phi}L(\beta(z),\beta'(z))}
\end{aligned}
$$

Objective functions
------
The problem can be presented as a two-step optimization problem, each of them working on a different objetive function: $B_1, B_2$ which depend on the hypothesis space $\mathcal{H}$, the representation space $\mathcal{N}$ and some hyperparameters $\alpha, \gamma, \lambda>0$
$$
\begin{aligned}
    B_1(\Phi, h) &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\left|h(\Phi(x_i),t_i)-y^F_i\right| \quad  \alert{\text{ Robust min. of factual errors}} \\
    & +\frac{\gamma}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\left|h(\Phi(x_i),1-t_i)-y^F_{j(i)}\right| \quad \alert{\text{ Robust min. of counterfactual e.}}  \\
    & +\alpha \text{disc}_{\mathcal{H}}(\hat{P}^F_\Phi, \hat{P}^C_\Phi) \quad \alert{\text{ Balancing factual/counter distributions of rep.}} \\
    B_2(\Phi, h) &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\left[h(\Phi(x_i),t_i)-y^F_i\right]^2 \quad \alert{\text{ Minimization of factual errors}} \\
    & + \lambda \norm{h}_{\mathcal{H}} \alert{\text{ Regularization}} 
\end{aligned}
$$

The algorithm
------
By finding the optimal representation $\Phi^$ we garantee some degree of similarity between the factual empirical distribution of the features. That representation helps construct the final learner $h^*$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
    (\Phi^*,g^*) &= \arg\min_{\Phi\in\mathcal{N}, g\in\mathcal{H}}B_1(\Phi, g) & \text{ Optimal robust and balanced rep.}\\
    h^* &= \arg\min_{h\in\mathcal{H}}B_2(\Phi^*, h) & \text{ Optimal final learner}
\end{aligned}
$$
